# Problem Definition and Problem Statement

## Problem Statement
Skilled workers pursuing Canadian PR through PNP (often with an Express Entry component) struggle to confidently decide the right pathway, prove eligibility with strong evidence, and sequence steps correctly under strict deadlines. The core pain is not form completion but interpretation: translating fragmented provincial and federal rules into personalized, defensible decisions. Because the stakes are high (refusal, delay, status risk), applicants pay consultants primarily for strategic judgment, risk reduction, and reassurance.

## Problem Definition

### User and Context
- Primary user: In-Canada economic immigration applicants pursuing PNP-led PR, especially employer-driven streams.
- Context: Multi-stage process across provincial and federal systems with changing criteria, document-heavy requirements, and hard timing gates.

### Struggling Moment
A temporary resident with a valid job offer is ready to apply but gets stuck when trying to answer: "Which stream is safest for my profile right now, what exact evidence will be accepted, and what must happen first so I do not miss a deadline or trigger refusal?" They can find rules online, but cannot confidently map those rules to their own case.

### What Makes This Problem Hard
- Rules are nonuniform across provinces and streams.
- Enhanced streams require coordinating provincial and federal logic.
- Evidence standards are strict but often not binary in practice (quality and defensibility matter).
- Deadlines and sequencing failures create costly resets.

### Current Workarounds
- Hiring paid representatives for strategy + quality control.
- Piecing together advice from forums/groups and government pages.
- Over-indexing on checklist completion while uncertainty remains unresolved.

## Evidence and Validation Notes
- Your discovery and JTBD notes consistently show recurring pain in four areas: eligibility interpretation, pathway comparison, evidence strength, and process sequencing.
- The materials indicate applicants value consultants mainly for consultative interpretation and risk mitigation, not only clerical form filling.
- The segment focus (PNP/economic pathways, often employer-driven) is high-volume and structurally complex, making the problem persistent rather than edge-case.

## Problem Type Split

### Customer Problem
Applicants cannot reliably convert public immigration rules into a personalized, high-confidence action plan that minimizes refusal/delay risk.

### Business Problem
Without solving the interpretation and confidence gap, any product risks becoming a low-value "form helper" that fails to compete with the consultancy layer users pay for.

## Desired User Outcome (If Solved)
- User can identify their best-fit pathway with clear tradeoffs.
- User can assemble evidence that is complete and defensible.
- User can follow the correct sequence and deadlines with high confidence.
- User feels informed and in control rather than anxious and guess-driven.

## Open Assumptions to Test Next
1. Which pain is most acute in willingness-to-pay terms: pathway choice, evidence confidence, or sequencing control?
2. What level of explanation and proof-checking is required before users trust software similarly to a human consultant?
3. For which sub-segment should this be solved first (for example, employer-driven in-Canada applicants vs broader PNP applicants)?
4. What measurable outcome should define success in early pilots (for example, confidence score increase, fewer completeness errors, faster decision-making)?

## Scope Guardrails
- Do not define this as a "form-filling" problem only.
- Do not start with AI features before validating the highest-stakes struggling moment.
- Treat confidence and defensibility as core value, not secondary UX polish.
